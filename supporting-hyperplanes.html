Supporting Hyperplanes and Convex Sets

A supporting hyperplane is a global tangent wall: it touches a convex set at the boundary, while the entire set stays on one side.

Upper envelopes Supporting hyperplanes

01

Definition

Let \(C\subseteq \mathbb R^n\) be a convex set. A hyperplane \(H=\{x:a^\top x=b\}\) supports \(C\) at a boundary point \(x_0\in C\) if

\[ a^\top x\le b \quad \text{for every }x\in C, \qquad a^\top x_0=b. \]

The first condition says the whole set is contained in one closed halfspace. The second says the hyperplane actually touches the set. The vector \(a\) is the normal vector: it points outward from the supporting wall.

03

Concrete Example: Unit Ball

For the unit ball \(C=\{x\in\mathbb R^n:\|x\|\le 1\}\), take a boundary point \(x_0\) with \(\|x_0\|=1\). The supporting hyperplane is

\[ x_0^\top x=1. \]

Why does it support the ball? By Cauchy-Schwarz,

\[ x_0^\top x\le \|x_0\|\,\|x\|\le 1 \quad \text{for every }x\in C. \]

Equality holds at \(x=x_0\). So the hyperplane touches the ball at \(x_0\), and the entire ball stays in the halfspace \(x_0^\top x\le 1\).

04

Why Convexity Matters

For a nonconvex set, a line can touch one piece while another piece lies on the other side. Convexity rules out that kind of folding. This is why supporting hyperplanes are global objects, not just local tangencies.

\[ \text{local boundary information} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \text{global halfspace containment}. \]

This is the same reason first-order conditions become so powerful in convex optimization. A gradient or subgradient gives a global inequality, not just a local approximation.

05

Connection to Convex Functions

A convex function \(f\) has a convex epigraph:

\[ \operatorname{epi}(f)=\{(x,t):t\ge f(x)\}. \]

Supporting hyperplanes of this convex set are exactly what produce supporting affine functions below \(f\):

\[ f(y)\ge f(x)+g^\top(y-x). \]

So the language of supporting hyperplanes for sets and the language of subgradients for functions are two views of the same geometric idea.
